# Technical Architecture & Loss Formulations

This document specifies the data representations, network architecture, and physical constraints that constitute ThermoKP. See `README.md` for a high-level overview and repository layout.

## 1. Sequence, Graph, and 3D Structural Data Representations (PyTorch Geometric)

The pipeline (`src/data/processors/generate_tensors.py`) produces `HeteroData` tensors that jointly encode protein sequence, 2D molecular graphs, and (Section 1b) 3D structural data for every enzyme-substrate reaction.

- **Protein Sequence**: a sequence of amino acid tokens, a per-residue catalytic-site flag (Section 1a), and a per-residue pretrained ESM2 embedding (Section 1a).
- **Ligand Graphs**: multi-substrate graphs built using RDKit, featurized with standard atom properties (element, hybridization, formal charge, aromaticity, Gasteiger partial charge, ring membership, heavy-atom degree, attached hydrogen count) and one-hot bond-type edge tensors. Element, hybridization, and bond-type vocabularies each carry a trailing "unknown" one-hot slot for values outside the closed vocabulary (e.g. metal-ion cofactors), so an out-of-vocabulary value is a distinct signal rather than an ambiguous all-zero encoding.
- **Covalent Edges**: intra-molecular covalent bonds (both intra-ligand and intra-co-substrate) use the `covalent_bond` edge type and carry a bond-type one-hot as an edge feature, consumed by the directed message-passing layer (Section 2) and reused, unmodified, by the 3D structural EGNN (Section 1b/2).

### 1a. Pretrained Sequence Representations & Catalytic/Reactivity Flags

Beyond structural one-hot features, the model draws on frozen pretrained embeddings (`src/data/processors/pretrained_embeddings.py`) for evolutionary and chemical context that 2D graphs and one-hot vocabularies alone cannot express:

- **Protein (ESM2-650M, `facebook/esm2_t33_650M_UR50D`)**: run once per unique UniProt sequence, producing 1280-dim per-residue embeddings.
- **Ligand/Co-Substrate (ChemBERTa-2, `DeepChem/ChemBERTa-77M-MLM`)**: run once per unique canonical SMILES (co-substrates use the dot-separated SMILES of their combined, disconnected RDKit `Mol`), producing a single 384-dim whole-molecule embedding via attention-mask-weighted mean pooling over token embeddings. This embedding is attached as a top-level, per-graph tensor (`data.ligand_embedding` / `data.co_substrate_embedding`, batched like `data.kcat`/`data.pH`) rather than broadcast onto every atom, since SMILES tokenization does not align 1:1 with RDKit atom indices and broadcasting would dilute the atom-level structural signal message passing depends on. It is fused into the pooled D-MPNN output by `MultimodalEncoder.mol_combine` (Section 2).
- **Protein catalytic-site flag**: each residue's feature vector includes a 2-dim flag (`get_catalytic_site_mask` in `pretrained_embeddings.py`) sourced from UniProt's curated "Active site"/"Binding site" sequence-feature annotations, gathered onto pocket residues by the same index used for the ESM2 embedding. All-zero for entries UniProt has no such annotations for - a supplementary signal, not a hard requirement.
- **Ligand reactivity signal**: each ligand/co-substrate atom's feature vector includes a continuous, molecule-relative reactivity pair - `electrophilic_score`/`nucleophilic_score`, the positive/negative parts of the atom's Gasteiger-charge z-score within its own molecule (`featurize_ligand` in `generate_tensors.py`).
- **Caching**: both embedding types, plus the protein catalytic-site mask and fetched PDB structures (Section 1b), are disk-cached under `data/cache/` (gitignored), keyed by UniProt ID or a SHA-256 hash of the SMILES, so repeated enzymes/ligands across kinetic records are only fetched/embedded once.
- **Point-mutation handling (mutate-then-chop)**: `brenda_parser.py` extracts single point mutations (e.g. `W95L`), tagging the record with a `mutation` code. `dataset_validator.py` validates the implied wild-type residue against the fetched sequence (checking both the raw index and the signal-peptide-offset-shifted index). `generate_tensors.py` applies the mutation to the raw precursor sequence first, then chops off the signal peptide before generating embeddings. Embeddings are cached under mutation-specific keys (`{uniprot_id}_{mutation}`).
- **Mutation descriptor**: a point mutation perturbs a single residue among hundreds, and that per-residue change does not reliably survive protein-level pooling (Section 2), which is dominated by whatever the rest of the sequence contributes regardless of how sharply one residue differs. Each record therefore carries a fixed-width mutation descriptor (`data.mutation_features`, assembled in `generate_tensors.py`) as a top-level per-graph tensor that reaches the encoder alongside pH/temperature (Section 2), bypassing pooling entirely. A wild-type record's descriptor is the exact zero vector - the neutral no-perturbation value - so wild-type and mutant tensors stay structurally symmetric rather than granting mutants an information channel wild-types lack (mutants are ~38% of records; the descriptor must not let the model treat them as a separate high-capacity regime). The seven components, summed over the sites of a multi-mutant, are: (1) mutation count; (2) the ESM2 masked-LM masked-marginal log-odds `log P(mutant | context) - log P(wild-type | context)` (`get_mutation_log_odds`, loaded via `AutoModelForMaskedLM`, masking the mutated position so the residue's own identity cannot leak in - the ESM-1v method of Meier et al.; cached per `(uniprot_id, position)`, since every substitution proposed at one position shares a single masked forward pass); (3-5) the wild-type→mutant deltas in Kyte-Doolittle hydropathy, side-chain volume, and net charge at pH 7; (6) the BLOSUM62 substitution score, a context-free evolutionary complement to the context-aware log-odds; and (7) the count of mutated positions coinciding with a UniProt-annotated catalytic/binding residue (a strong mechanistic signal, since active-site substitutions dominate turnover effects). All components derive from residue-identity lookup tables or already-cached ESM2 quantities, at no additional network or model cost.

### 1b. 3D Structural Representations

`src/data/processors/geometry_processor.py` augments each `HeteroData` tensor with a 3D structural view of the reaction, independent of and additive to the 2D/sequence representations above:

- **Structure acquisition**: an AlphaFold DB (`AF-{uniprot_id}-F1-model_v6.pdb`) structure is downloaded per UniProt ID, falling back to an ESM Atlas single-sequence folding prediction (`fetch_esmfold_pdb`) when AlphaFold has no model for that ID. Both sources cache to `data/cache/pdbs/{uniprot_id}.pdb`. `dataset_validator.py` checks structure availability upfront (via either source) and drops entries with neither, so `generate_tensors.py` never has to handle a missing structure downstream.
- **Pocket prediction & cropping**: [P2Rank](https://github.com/rdk/p2rank) (`-c alphafold` config, tuned for pLDDT rather than crystallographic B-factors) predicts the highest-ranked ligand-binding pocket; atoms within a 10 Å radius of its center are retained as the `protein_pocket_atoms` node type. This is a separate, spatially-restricted view of the protein from the full-chain `protein_sequence` node type (Section 1) used by the 2D branch.
- **Pocket featurization reuses the 2D pipeline's outputs**: rather than a second ESM2 forward pass, each retained atom's per-residue ESM2 embedding and catalytic-site flag are gathered from the already-computed, mature-sequence-aligned tensors (Section 1a) by mapping the atom's AlphaFold structure index to the mature-sequence index via the signal-peptide offset. The one-hot residue identity is read from the (possibly mutated) mature sequence at that index, not from the atom's own wild-type PDB `resname`, so a point mutation is reflected in the pocket's identity feature even though the coordinates still come from the wild-type structure (no mutant-specific structure exists).
- **Ligand/co-substrate 3D conformers**: RDKit ETKDG embedding + MMFF94 optimization generates 3D coordinates for the same `ligand_atoms`/`co_substrate_atoms` RDKit `Mol` objects the 2D D-MPNN already featurizes (Section 1) - the atom-level structural features (element, charges, reactivity scores, etc.) and `covalent_bond` edges are not duplicated, only a `pos` tensor is added onto the existing node types.
- **`interacts_with` edges**: bipartite, KD-tree-derived edges (6 Å cutoff) connect `protein_pocket_atoms` ↔ `ligand_atoms` and `ligand_atoms` ↔ `co_substrate_atoms`, giving the 3D EGNN (Section 2) a proximity-based message-passing topology no 2D representation can express.
- **Failure handling**: PDB download, pocket prediction, cropping, and conformer generation failures raise and drop the entry via the same try/except (`failed_tensors.txt`) `generate_tensors.py` already uses for 2D failures - not silently zero-filled.

## 2. Multimodal Encoder & Readout

The core architecture (`src/encoders/multimodal_encoder.py`) fuses a sequence/2D-graph branch with a 3D structural branch, both feeding a single unified kinetics head:

**2D branch (sequence/graph):**
1. **Protein Adapter (`ProteinAdapterEncoder`)**: a lightweight residual adapter over frozen ESM2 embeddings - project ESM2 to `hidden_channels`, add a trainable amino-acid-identity embedding, and refine through a small bottleneck MLP. ESM2 already encodes per-residue evolutionary and positional context via its own self-attention layers, so no additional self-attention stack is trained here.
2. **Ligand and Co-Substrate D-MPNNs (`DMPNNEncoder`)**: 2D molecular graphs are processed by a Directed Message Passing Neural Network operating over `covalent_bond` edges, using only atom-level structural features (element, hybridization, charges, reactivity scores). Each D-MPNN's pooled output is fused with that molecule's whole-molecule ChemBERTa embedding by `mol_combine` (shared between ligand and co-substrate), which projects the embedding to `hidden_channels` and combines it with the pooled atom-level representation through a small nonlinear layer.
3. **Substrate-Conditioned Pooling (`SubstrateConditionedPooling`)**: each substrate's fused representation queries the protein's per-residue states via attention, supplementing the static catalytic-site-annotation pooling (which is frequently absent for poorly-characterized enzymes) with a signal specific to the bound substrate. `protein_pool_combine` fuses the catalytic-site-pooled and both substrate-conditioned-pooled representations into the final protein representation.

**3D branch (structural, `StructuralEGNNEncoder`):**
4. **Pretrained-embedding adapter**: `protein_pocket_atoms`' raw feature tensor (one-hot block + gathered catalytic flags + gathered ESM2 embedding, Section 1b) is split at its trailing `esm_dim` columns; the ESM2 slice passes through a small adapter (`protein_pocket_adapter`, output width `egnn_adapter_dim`) before rejoining the one-hot slice and being projected to `egnn_hidden_channels` - keeping the 1280-dim embedding from numerically dominating the ~27-dim structural one-hot once concatenated. `ligand_atoms`/`co_substrate_atoms` are projected directly (no embedding slice to adapt - their raw features are the same 2D atom-level tensors, no broadcast ChemBERTa embedding baked in).
5. **Heterogeneous EGNN (`HeteroEGNNLayer`/`HeteroEGNNMessagePassing`)**: E(n)-equivariant bipartite message passing over `interacts_with` (protein pocket ↔ ligand ↔ co-substrate proximity, Section 1b) and `covalent_bond` (reusing the 2D branch's own ligand/co-substrate edges) routes. The squared pairwise distance is normalized by the interaction cutoff (36 Å², i.e. 6 Å²) before entering each edge MLP to match the roughly unit-scale node embeddings. The final layer skips its coordinate update (`update_pos=False`), since pooling (next step) reads only node features and no subsequent layer would consume it.
6. **Gated-attention pooling (`AttentionalAggregation`)**: each of the three 3D node types (`protein_pocket_atoms`/`ligand_atoms`/`co_substrate_atoms`) is pooled independently via a learned attention gate, rather than uniform mean pooling, so catalytically relevant pocket atoms can dominate their node type's pooled vector.

**Fusion**: the 2D branch's three pooled vectors (protein/ligand/co-substrate, width `hidden_channels` each) and the 3D branch's three pooled vectors (protein pocket/ligand/co-substrate, width `egnn_hidden_channels` each) are all **concatenated**, not summed, into the final graph representation - always in this fixed order, with an exact zero vector substituted for any node type/branch absent from a given graph (an absent co-substrate on either branch, or the entire 3D branch when a tensor carries no 3D structural data - `StructuralEGNNEncoder.forward` detects a missing `protein_pocket_atoms` node type and returns zeros rather than raising). Concatenation keeps each node type's and each branch's signal in its own disjoint subspace. Finally, these representations are concatenated with the scaled pH scalar, the scaled temperature scalar, and the seven-component mutation descriptor (Section 1a) to form the final `concat_dim` representation (`hidden_channels * 3 + egnn_hidden_channels * 3 + 2 + 7`) - a single source of truth read by both physical heads and the baseline head so the width cannot drift. pH and temperature are centered/scaled (`(pH - 7) / 7`, `T / 298.15`), and each mutation-descriptor component is divided by a fixed per-component magnitude (`mutation_feature_scale`) before concatenation, since the pooled representations are roughly unit-scale (LayerNorm-derived) and each raw metadata value would otherwise dominate the heads' first layer by numeric magnitude alone.

An absent co-substrate must still produce an exactly-zero contribution: `mol_combine`'s own bias/LayerNorm terms would otherwise leak a nonzero value from an all-zero input, so its output is explicitly masked by whether a co-substrate embedding is actually present.

### Dropout Policy

Dropout is split into two tiers:
- **Body dropout** (default 0.3): applied throughout the protein adapter, D-MPNN, and 3D EGNN message-passing stacks.
- **Head dropout** (default 0.1): applied only within `kinetics_head` and `energy_net`, the MLPs that directly output $k_1$, $k_{-1}$, and $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$. Because the Eyring relation (Section 4) exponentiates $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$, roughly 1.36 kcal/mol of prediction noise costs a full decade of $k_{cat}$ error - the heads use a lower dropout rate than the body to avoid injecting that noise directly into the scored output.

`kinetics_head` (`concat_dim` → `hidden_channels` → 2) is kept deliberately narrow: $k_1$/$k_{-1}$ have no direct supervision (only kcat/Km targets exist), so extra capacity here only gives the optimizer more room to satisfy $L_{Km}$ via $k_1$/$k_{-1}$ compensation (Section 5) rather than a genuine fit. `energy_net` (`concat_dim` → `concat_dim // 2` → 2 with LayerNorm) is kept similarly narrow: even though $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$ is directly supervised via $L_{kcat}$, unlike $k_1$/$k_{-1}$, more capacity here still risks fitting training-set idiosyncrasies rather than the underlying physics. The 3D branch itself is sized smaller than the 2D branch (`egnn_hidden_channels=32`, `egnn_num_layers=3` vs. `hidden_channels=64` for the sequence/D-MPNN branch) - it augments the already-capable 2D representation with geometric context rather than replacing it, so it does not need matching capacity.

## 3. Hard Physical Rate Constraints

Rather than penalizing physically implausible rate constants with a soft physics-loss term, ThermoKP enforces two universal physical ceilings structurally, through bounded output parameterizations - the model is architecturally incapable of representing an unphysical rate constant, regardless of what the network predicts.

- **Diffusion limit on $k_1$** (`src/encoders/multimodal_encoder.py`): a bimolecular association rate constant in solution cannot exceed the Smoluchowski diffusion-controlled encounter limit. `kinetics_head` outputs a logit that is squashed via
  $$k_1 = k_{1,\text{diff}} \cdot \sigma(\text{logit}), \quad k_{1,\text{diff}} = 10^{10}\ \text{M}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$$
  This is a fixed constant, not temperature-dependent - it neglects the modest solvent-viscosity variation with $T$ over typical assay ranges (~20-45°C), immaterial at the order-of-magnitude scale this ceiling operates at.

- **Eyring/TST speed limit on $k_{-1}$** (`src/encoders/multimodal_encoder.py`): dissociation is itself a unimolecular elementary step and cannot exceed $k_BT/h$, the transition-state-theory ceiling for any unimolecular rate constant. Computed at the batch's actual temperature $T$:
  $$k_{-1} = \frac{k_B T}{h} \cdot \sigma(\text{logit})$$

- **Eyring/TST speed limit on $k_{cat}$ via $\Delta G^{\ddagger} \geq 0$** (`src/physics/thermodynamics.py`): the activation free energy is passed through softplus before entering the Eyring equation (Section 4), structurally guaranteeing $\Delta G^{\ddagger} \geq 0$ - a negative activation barrier is unphysical, since a barrierless transition state is already the fastest a reaction can proceed. This in turn guarantees $k_{cat} \leq \kappa \cdot (k_BT/h) \leq k_BT/h$.

Both $\sigma(\cdot)$ (sigmoid) and softplus saturate smoothly for any real-valued logit, so no exponent clamp is needed to prevent float32 overflow.

Constraining the rate constants structurally removes any need for assumed concentrations, experimental initial conditions, or dynamic loss weighting schemes, ensuring numerical stability.

## 4. Thermodynamic Enforcements

### A. Arrhenius / Eyring Integration

The model does not output $k_{cat}$ as an arbitrary scalar. Instead, the encoder outputs the activation free energy ($\Delta G^{\ddagger}$, passed through softplus per Section 3). The differentiable layer explicitly calculates $k_{cat}$ using the input temperature variable $T$ (in Kelvin).

$$ k_{cat} = \kappa \cdot \frac{k_B T}{h} \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta G^{\ddagger}}{R T}\right) $$

Where $\kappa$ is a learned transmission coefficient (sigmoid-bounded to $(0, 1)$), $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant, $h$ is Planck's constant, and $R$ is the gas constant.

The `energy_net` final layer bias is initialized to **15.0 kcal/mol** (pre-softplus) to place the initial $k_{cat}$ prediction near $10^2\ \text{s}^{-1}$ (the biological median) rather than the uninformative random-initialization value of $\sim10^{12}\ \text{s}^{-1}$. All computation uses float64 internally (returning the original tensor dtype) to avoid underflow when exponentiating very negative arguments; the exponent argument is clamped to a minimum of -700 (float64 underflows `exp()` to exactly 0.0 only around -744) rather than a tight bound, since a tighter clamp would zero the gradient for any example whose true $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$ sits above it - plausible for slow or catalytically-impaired mutant enzymes, which this dataset includes. Since `softplus` guarantees $\Delta G^{\ddagger} \geq 0$, the exponent $-\Delta G^{\ddagger}/(RT)$ is always $\leq 0$, so no upper clamp is required.

`EyringArrheniusLayer.forward` and `ThermoKPModel.forward` both accept an opt-in `return_components: bool = False` argument; when `True`, they additionally return the $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$/$\kappa$ pair actually used to compute $k_{cat}$ above, rather than just $k_{cat}$ itself. Defaulting to `False` leaves the plain-tensor/3-tuple return used by the training loop and `src/evaluation/evaluate_dataset.py` untouched; the dashboard (Section 7) is the only caller that requests it.

### B. Briggs-Haldane Constraint

The macroscopic Michaelis constant ($K_m$) is derived from the predicted microscopic rate parameters ($k_1$, $k_{-1}$, $k_{cat}$) using the Briggs-Haldane equation. The model enforces this relationship by penalizing the log-space root mean squared error (RMSE) against empirical $K_m$ targets:

$$K_m = \frac{k_{-1} + k_{cat}}{k_1}$$
$$L_{Km} = \sqrt{\left\| \log_{10}(K_m) - \log_{10}(target\_K_m) \right\|^2 + \epsilon}$$

## 5. Multi-Task Loss Function & Optimizer

The optimization objective is the data-fitting loss, computed in log-space to stabilize gradients across exponentially varying scales. Each component is an RMSE, not a raw MSE, so its magnitude reads directly as log10 units of error (e.g. `L_kcat = 0.3` means k_cat predictions are typically off by a factor of $10^{0.3} \approx 2\times$):

$$L_{total} = kcat\_weight \cdot \sqrt{\left\| \log_{10}(k_{cat}) - \log_{10}(target\_k_{cat}) \right\|^2 + \epsilon} + \sqrt{\left\| \log_{10}(K_m) - \log_{10}(target\_K_m) \right\|^2 + \epsilon}$$

`K_m` is algebraically derived from all three predicted rates ($k_1$, $k_{-1}$, $k_{cat}$ - 3 degrees of freedom against 1 target), while $k_{cat}$ is a direct function of only the Eyring layer's two outputs ($\Delta G^{\ddagger}$, $\kappa$ - 2 degrees of freedom against 1 target). Equal 1:1 weighting lets the optimizer shrink `L_Km` via $k_1$/$k_{-1}$ compensation without the $k_{cat}$ pathway needing to improve. `kcat_weight` (default 2.5, `PINNMultiTaskLoss` constructor / `--kcat_weight` CLI arg) counteracts this by weighting `L_kcat`'s contribution more heavily than `L_Km`'s in the combined total that gets backpropagated. A small `eps` (1e-12) guards against a literal `log(0)`/div-by-0, and is reused inside each RMSE's `sqrt` since its gradient diverges as the underlying MSE approaches zero; it is kept well below the smallest physically plausible target (Km can legitimately be nanomolar for high-affinity enzymes) so it never distorts a genuine small measurement.

### Periodic Validation Reporting

Every `PINNTrainer.DETAILED_VALIDATION_INTERVAL` epochs (default 10), and once more after training ends, `PINNTrainer` prints an extended validation report for both $k_{cat}$ and $K_m$: log-space RMSE (identical to the training loss, for direct comparability), $R^2$ against the validation target mean, and p1mag - the percentage of predictions within one order of magnitude of their target ($|\log_{10}(pred) - \log_{10}(target)| \le 1$). The post-training report re-validates the Pareto checkpoint (`models/best_model.pth`) if one was ever saved during the run, or the final `ema_model` weights if the Pareto gate never triggered.

### EMA Shadow Model

`PINNTrainer.ema_model` is a frozen copy of the live model whose weights track an exponential moving average of the live weights, updated once per optimizer step (`_update_ema`) - never receiving gradients itself. Validation and every saved checkpoint use `ema_model`, not the live model, since the live weights' epoch-to-epoch trajectory is noisy enough (particularly for the exponentially-sensitive $k_{cat}$/Eyring pathway) that evaluating them directly can make real progress look like random fluctuation. `ema_decay` (default 0.999) is applied once per mini-batch.

### Train/Validation Split & Sampling

`train_val_split` builds an approximately 90/10 train/validation split grouped by `(uniprot_id, primary substrate)` reaction identity, not by protein alone - the same protein can appear on both sides, but the same protein/substrate reaction never does, since two near-identical records of one reaction (differing only in pH/temperature/mutation) would otherwise let the model partly memorize a validation reaction instead of generalizing. Substrate identity is derived from each record's `ligand_embedding` tensor rather than a stored SMILES string, since `get_ligand_embedding` caches its ChemBERTa output keyed by the exact canonical SMILES - the same substrate always yields a bit-identical tensor. Novel-protein generalization is measured separately by the whole-enzyme benchmark holdout carved out before tensorization (`src/data/processors/clean_records.py`); grouping this split by protein alone would otherwise exclude some proteins from the training split entirely. Within the training split, `train_loader` samples with replacement via a `WeightedRandomSampler` weighted by inverse per-protein record count, so no single over-represented protein (BRENDA/SABIO-RK are long-tailed) dominates gradient updates.

### Optimization Strategy

The model trains under a single AdamW optimizer for the entire schedule:
- **AdamW**: parameters are split along two axes - physical role (`kinetics_head`/`thermo` get a lower LR, since they directly produce the exponentially-sensitive rate constants) and dimensionality (1-D parameters - LayerNorm and bias terms - are excluded from weight decay, since decaying a normalization scale/shift or bias doesn't serve the same regularization purpose as decaying a weight matrix). The LR schedule is a monotonic `LambdaLR`: linear warmup over the first 2% of epochs, rapid decay to 20% of peak through 10% of epochs total, then cosine decay to 0.01% of peak - deliberately monotonic (no restarts) to avoid reintroducing violent LR jumps into the physical parameters. Base learning rates start at $5\times 10^{-3}$ for network parameters and $1.5\times 10^{-3}$ for physical layers. Gradients are clipped (`clip_grad_norm_`, `max_norm=1.0`) once per step.
- **Auto-Stop**: `PINNTrainer` fits a linear slope to the validation total loss over a `early_stop_window_frac`-of-`max_epochs` lookback window (default 0.2) each epoch, and stops training once that slope rises above `-early_stop_slope_epsilon` (default 1e-5) - a plateau or a rising validation loss, since the loss is pure data-fitting with no annealed physics-loss schedule and so the landscape is stable from epoch 0. The threshold is kept tight so the stop only fires on a genuinely flat-or-rising slope rather than one still slowly improving, ending training early rather than running out the full `max_epochs` schedule for no further gain.

## 6. Diagnostic & Ablation Modes

### Training-Time Diagnostics

Two opt-in `PINNTrainer`/`PINNMultiTaskLoss` modes exist purely to disambiguate the cause of a stalled validation metric, not for production training:
- **`kcat_only`** (`PINNMultiTaskLoss` constructor / `--kcat_only` CLI arg): drops `L_Km` from the backpropagated total entirely (`L_kcat` is still computed and logged). Isolates whether $k_{cat}$'s own gradient path is being degraded by $L_{Km}$'s backward pass through the shared trunk (via $k_1$/$k_{-1}$), as opposed to a representation ceiling independent of $K_m$. Also bypasses the `km_tolerance` Pareto-checkpoint gate, since $k_1$/$k_{-1}$ never receive gradient in this mode.
- **LR-restart** (`lr_restart_patience`/`lr_restart_target_epoch`/`lr_restart_max`, all opt-in via `--lr_restart_*` CLI args, disabled by default): rewinds the AdamW LR schedule (to `lr_restart_target_epoch`, default 0 - the very start, replaying the full warmup ramp) after `lr_restart_patience` epochs of stalled validation $k_{cat}$ loss, up to `lr_restart_max` times. Tests whether an apparent plateau is an escapable optimization artifact rather than a genuine representation ceiling.

### Non-Physics Baseline

`train_baseline_nn.py` (`BaselineNNModel`/`BaselineNNLoss`) is a separate training pipeline used purely as an ablation control. It reuses `MultimodalEncoder` unmodified - the exact same fused 2D+3D `concat_rep` the Eyring layer consumes - but replaces the thermodynamics layer and Briggs-Haldane $K_m$ derivation with a plain MLP head regressing $\log_{10}(k_{cat})$ and $\log_{10}(K_m)$ directly. Its MLP head matches the combined depth and parameter count of the PINN's physical heads, and it is trained using the identical `PINNTrainer` loop (AdamW with slope-based auto-stop, EMA shadow model) against the identical reaction-grouped 90/10 split. Since it shares the PINN's exact input tensors, encoder, parameter budget, and optimizer setup, a similar error floor implicates the data/tensors/encoder rather than the physics constraints; a substantially better fit implicates the Eyring/Briggs-Haldane architecture instead.

## 7. Zero-Shot Inference & Dataset Evaluation

### `thermokp.py`

The single inference entry point, taking a UniProt ID (optionally with `/`-separated point-mutation codes), a substrate list (the first entry is the primary substrate $K_m$ is reported against, the rest are combined as co-substrates; each entry may be a database-style chemical name or a raw SMILES string), and assay pH/temperature. `build_enzyme_substrate_graph` reproduces the training-time featurization path one-to-one - UniProt sequence fetch, mutate-then-chop mutation handling (`apply_mutations`, `generate_tensors.py`), ESM2/ChemBERTa embeddings, 2D D-MPNN graphs, and the mandatory 3D structural branch (AlphaFold/ESMFold + P2Rank; not an optional fallback here, since every training tensor carries it) - entirely in memory, without writing to `data/processed/tensors/`. `load_model` resolves a checkpoint (explicit path, else `models/best_model.pth` → `final_model.pth`) into either `ThermoKPModel` or `BaselineNNModel` (`--model_type`), and `predict_log_kinetics` dispatches the forward pass (Eyring + Briggs-Haldane for the PINN, direct log10 regression for the baseline) into a common `(log10_kcat, log10_km)` return shape. Every anticipated failure (unresolvable UniProt ID/chemical, mismatched mutation, sequence exceeding ESM2's context window, unattainable structure, missing/incompatible checkpoint) raises `ThermoKPError` carrying a unique, stable `TKP-NNN` error code rather than degrading silently, so an interactive caller (a dashboard) can report exactly what went wrong.

### `dashboard/` — Streamlit Inference Dashboard

A local `streamlit run dashboard/app.py` demo of the zero-shot inference path above, built on `thermokp.py` rather than duplicating its featurization logic. `dashboard/inference_helpers.py`'s `predict_with_components` calls `thermokp.build_enzyme_substrate_graph` and then `ThermoKPModel.forward`/`EyringArrheniusLayer.forward`'s opt-in `return_components=True` path (Section 4.A) to additionally surface the model's actual internal $k_1$, $k_{-1}$, $\Delta G^{\ddagger}$, and $\kappa$ - not an externally back-derived approximation - alongside $k_{cat}$, $K_m$, and the derived catalytic efficiency $k_a = k_{cat}/K_m$; the baseline model exposes none of these since it has no Eyring layer. `run_batch` applies the same call per row of an uploaded CSV, capturing any `ThermoKPError` into a per-row `error` column rather than aborting the run. `dashboard/structure_view.py` renders an interactive py3Dmol cartoon of the queried enzyme's AlphaFold/ESMFold structure, reusing `geometry_processor.py`'s `download_pdb`/`predict_pocket`/`crop_atoms` to highlight the P2Rank pocket, `generate_tensors.resolve_mutation_positions` (factored out of `apply_mutations` for this reuse) to highlight any queried mutation site(s), and an illustrative overlay of the primary substrate's freshly generated 3D conformer recentered on the pocket - not a docked pose, since the model performs no docking.

### `src/evaluation/evaluate_dataset.py`

Reports log-space RMSE, $R^2$, and p1mag (identical formulas to `PINNTrainer._regression_metrics`) overall, per percentile-of-error bin (quartiles plus finer 90th/95th/99th-percentile tail bins), and split into wild-type-only/mutant-only subsections, for both $k_{cat}$ and $K_m$ - including the full wild-type/mutant log-space absolute-error distributions (`wild_type_abs_log_errors`/`mutant_abs_log_errors`), not just their summary statistics, for later plotting as a density histogram. With no `--split` given, runs both `val` and `benchmark` as two separate evaluations (each summarized, logged, and saved under its own key) by default - a quick local sanity check followed by the slower true zero-shot benchmark. `--split benchmark` evaluates the withheld benchmark holdout by calling `thermokp.py`'s live inference pipeline per row, since that split was never tensorized; `--split train`/`val`/`all` instead evaluate the cached tensors under `data/processed/tensors/` directly (reconstructing the reaction-grouped 90/10 split via `train_val_split` for `train`/`val`, or the full training set for `all`), with no live featurization or network calls. Sequence identity cutoffs (<=99%, <=80%, <=60%, <=40%) are computed dynamically against the specific model's training set (cached in `data/cache_eval/benchmark_seq_ids_{model_type}.json`). `--model_type {thermokp,dlkcat,unikp,catpred,unikineg,all}` selects which checkpoint architecture to load, where `thermokp` sequentially benchmarks PINN and Baseline, and external models read offline-generated CSV predictions from `data/external_predictions/`. Each run's metrics and per-record predictions are merged into the single shared `data/results/eval.json`, keyed by `"{split}_{model_type}"` (written atomically, preserving other runs' entries) rather than scattered across separate per-run files, for later visualization (parity plots, error concentrations, and cross-model generalization comparisons).
